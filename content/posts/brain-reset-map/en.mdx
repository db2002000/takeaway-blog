## 1. The Possibility of Change

Neurons have the ability to change and adapt. The more a message is repeated, the stronger the pathway becomes—it transforms from a bumpy dirt road into a highway. And since the brain prefers to conserve energy, it tends to “cruise” along these well-trodden paths.

This is why we often follow a very familiar line of thinking, and why it’s difficult to develop or break a habit or mindset without deliberate effort.

The good news is that we can both break down old pathways and create new ones. A key player in this process is <Concept id="bdnf">BDNF</Concept>, which can be thought of as “brain food” for neurons; it makes it easier for the brain to establish new pathways.

BDNF is abundant in childhood, which is why we naturally absorb so much—language, movements, habits, and even our worldviews form rapidly. But even as adults, the brain retains the ability to build new circuits, and there are many things we can do to boost BDNF.

Now we have a new perspective. Next, I’ll explain what’s happening behind the scenes while sharing strategies for managing these states.

## 2. Breaking the Cycle

### Let’s Use Stress as a Starting Point

Life is always full of challenges, and moderate stress can help us cope better with various situations. But when acute stress doesn’t subside and turns into chronic stress, or when external demands exceed what we can handle, the body may enter <Concept id="low-power-mode"> “low-power mode”</Concept>. In this state, the brain prioritizes “survival” over “changing the situation.” We become easily fatigued, prone to self-blame, and hyper-alert, unable to think clearly. These states further exacerbate our difficulties, leaving us feeling stuck.

I wonder if you often find yourself sighing—this is actually your body’s way of trying to reset your breathing rhythm. When facing a stressor, you can actively use a <Concept id="physiological-sigh">physiological sigh</Concept> to help quickly reset your stress response.

The second approach is to find an activity that allows you to fully immerse yourself in it. Learn to recover from stress and prevent manageable stress from turning into chronic stress.

Moreover, our perception of stress and the way we think about our stress responses influence both our physiological and emotional reactions to stressful events.

If none of these methods appeal to you, then look for ways to <Concept id="amygdala">calm the amygdala</Concept> and restore the normal functioning of the <Concept id="parasympathetic">parasympathetic nervous system</Concept>. When it comes to exercise, I highly recommend starting with a walk.

---

### When Emotions Arise

When we’re under stress, tired, or experiencing intense emotions, the <Concept id="prefrontal-cortex">prefrontal cortex</Concept>—which is responsible for rational thinking—becomes less effective. We find it difficult to think clearly and are more likely to be swept away by our emotions.

When intense emotions strike:

1. **<Concept id="physiological-sigh">Physiological sighing</Concept>** is an excellent tool for restoring prefrontal cortex function.
2. **Step outside** — By broadening your perspective and removing yourself from the environment that triggered the emotion, you give your logical mind space to better analyze the situation.

Other Emotions:

Identifying emotions allows us to clearly understand our true feelings. Naming these emotions activates the prefrontal cortex, helping us regain control of the situation.

Emotions themselves are not the problem; what truly traps us is the bias we develop toward ourselves when subjected to prolonged emotions and stress.

### Anxiety

We often worry: What if I haven’t achieved anything by 30? What if I can’t find a job? Did I just say something wrong? These thoughts aren’t inherently problematic; they arise from our desire to solve problems. The danger lies in constantly repeating these worries and <Concept id="rumination">ruminating</Concept> on them, which turns them into anxiety. We easily fall into ruminative thinking, repeatedly talking to ourselves with negative self-talk. Gradually, the brain establishes new communication patterns, and different brain regions begin to frequently exchange negative signals. This shapes our subsequent beliefs, which in turn determine how we make decisions and take action.

A couple of days ago, I caught myself on the verge of ruminating on a traumatic event, but I decided not to interrupt it—I wanted to see what would happen. As a result, my sleep that night was like this: the terrifying part was that even in that half-asleep, half-awake state, my mind was still consumed by that event.

In this state, the body perceives tension as safe, and relaxation becomes a dangerous thing. Anxiety isn’t always just psychological; it can also manifest physically, such as through a racing heart, muscle tension, or restlessness. This is closely related to the <Concept id="amygdala">amygdala</Concept>. If it becomes overactive, we’re prone to remaining in a state of prolonged tension, anticipating risks, and being hyper-alert—even when there’s nothing to fear.

Persistent anxiety is also likely due to it entering a <Concept id="habit-loop">habit loop</Concept>. When a trigger appears, the brain automatically triggers a cycle of repetitive thinking: imagining the worst-case scenario, searching for answers, and attempting to regain a sense of control to alleviate the perceived threat. While this temporarily relieves anxiety, it reinforces the loop. We should be particularly wary of whether, after the anxiety temporarily subsides, we immediately turn to social media—which releases dopamine, prompting the brain to want to repeat the entire process.

To break the anxiety loop, the first step is to identify the cue. If we can recognize it before entering the loop, we have a much better chance of stopping the compulsive thinking. With continuous awareness and practice, this loop will gradually weaken.

Another crucial question is: How do we perceive these events? Is it really as I imagine it to be? This is the core of cognitive intervention—not suppressing thoughts, but changing how we interpret events.

Going for a walk is, in my opinion, the best place to start. As your eyes scan left and right, you unconsciously activate the frontoparietal network, temporarily pushing the emotional brain into the background. My own transformation began with walking—at the time, I was simply trying to solve my insomnia, but the changes it brought were far greater than I had anticipated.

Recently, what has helped me the most is Jon Kabat-Zinn’s meditation course. Every day, I open it up, sit down, listen to him speak, and use the pauses in his speech to tune into myself.

Visualization is another tool—we can rehearse in advance: in a specific situation, how might I feel, and how might I react? Otherwise, when we actually face that situation, it’s easy to fall back into familiar automatic patterns. You can also write down what’s troubling you to find a breakthrough, or tell yourself: “I’ve already thought this far; next time, I won’t have to start from scratch.”
