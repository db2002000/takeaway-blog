Last year, I resolved to live a more disciplined life and learn the skills I was interested in. But I’m neither particularly self-disciplined nor fond of grueling perseverance, so I kept wondering if there was an easier way to achieve my goals. After giving up on learning Spanish three times, this time not only did I pick it up effortlessly, but it also brought about many changes in my life.

## The Habit Loop

Typically, a cue triggers a thought or action, which is then reinforced by a reward—this is the <Concept id="habit-loop">habit loop</Concept>. Next, we’ll use this way the brain works to design our routines. Fortunately, this mechanism is the same for everyone, though each person has their own preferences.

Another key point is to lower the activation threshold and raise the interference threshold to reduce mental effort and shorten the decision-making process.

---

## Target Behavior

One major difference this time is that I won’t be spending time while doing these activities wondering what kind of person I am. So at the very beginning, the smaller and easier the target behavior is to start with, the better—don’t give yourself a chance to underestimate yourself. At first, I only opened Duolingo and went for a walk every day. As I became more accustomed to this rhythm, I began to consider upgrading or expanding my tasks.

Alternatively, the more specific the behavior, the better. For example, with video editing, I can focus on learning specific effects that interest me one at a time.

I don’t believe you have to suffer or push yourself to the limit to bring about change. Even now, most of my tasks remain small, which increases the likelihood of consistent repetition—and that’s when accumulation or the compounding effect kicks in. Continuous repetition strengthens this neural loop, making it highly energy-efficient for the brain.

If you set a big goal but don’t believe you can change, the whole process becomes exhausting and draining. What are the chances of success under those conditions? It also makes you more likely to avoid mistakes and failures, reinforcing negative beliefs about yourself: “See, I really can’t do it.”

## Designing Cues

Now that we have a target behavior, the next step is to design the cues that trigger it. There are many types of cues; I’ll start by sharing the ones I use.

Although I love studying with friends, it’s hard to coordinate schedules. Scenarios or objects, however, are more controllable cues. We can link them to the target behavior and eliminate distractions.

Before designing cues, ask yourself three questions:

- What will the target behavior require?
- What might distract me?
- Where will the behavior take place?

My desk is the kind where I can just plop down and start studying. The books I want to read are always open, and I basically never play games here.

A yoga mat laid out, supplements on the dining table, reminders on my phone—things like these can all serve as cues. You can refer to the [Guide to Using Reminders](/zh/posts/apple-reminders-guide).

Beyond that, there’s one method that has helped me the most—I’d say I wouldn’t be where I am today without it.

The **mobile environment** is even more important for people today. I want it to be a good tool that helps me, not one that hinders me.

So I set it to automatically display the appropriate home screen based on the schedule I’ve set. I keep only relevant apps or widgets on the home screen, so I think, “Okay, I’ll just tap that,” and I place them where they’re easiest to access. I remove all distractions from the screen.

To further reduce the barrier to getting started, I’ve set up many automated actions, such as automatically playing podcasts or opening a specific video I need for studying. Basically, anything that can be automated, I’ve automated.

## Rewards

The shorter the interval between the behavior and the reward, the better the effect.

These behaviors may not provide an immediate dopamine hit like scrolling through a phone; in most cases, the results aren’t visible in the short term. The brain may instinctively revert to the safety and familiarity of negative thought patterns. So we can proactively design rewards to strengthen the new neural pathways.

Actually, boosting intrinsic motivation is a great starting point. I’ll use the chemicals it produces as my reward, gradually making it something I can stick with without draining my willpower.

Ask yourself these three questions:

- What makes me feel like I can do this?
- What makes me feel like I have a choice?
- What makes me feel like I’m not alone?

My first reward is the [tracking system](/zh/posts/personal-tracking-system) I shared earlier, which visualizes competence and autonomy. Even without the system, simply checking off items is highly effective. The second is a reminder app; it serves both as a prompt and a reward, and since it’s part of my personal database, it sometimes becomes part of the target behavior itself.

You can take inspiration from achievement systems in games, or take a bath or stretch after exercising to use your body’s release of endorphins to reinforce the loop. Or simply tell yourself, “I’m awesome,” after you’re done.

Finally, I’d like to offer a suggestion: place the reward naturally after the behavior—don’t rely on the reward to drive the behavior, but use it to reinforce the loop. If the reward is too prominent, it’s easy to end up doing the task solely for the reward.

Additionally, when we adopt a growth mindset, the areas of the brain associated with effort and perseverance become more active, and the act of putting in the effort itself becomes rewarding.
