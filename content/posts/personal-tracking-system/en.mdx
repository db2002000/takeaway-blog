It wasn’t until I started tracking my own behavior that I realized I simply hadn’t taken the time to really understand myself.

For example, I now know how many cups of coffee I can have in a day and what time is the latest I can drink it without affecting my sleep. By keeping a record, I’ve gradually come to understand myself much better.

In the past, I often wondered if I was capable of doing something. But once you start recording things, many of those doubts actually disappear. That’s because tracking is a source of my sense of competence and autonomy—two key factors that fuel intrinsic motivation. To put it simply: I can do this, and I’m the one in charge.

For me, tracking serves another crucial purpose: it provides a basis for optimizing my life. For instance, it helps me identify which activities I’ve consistently neglected, and which ones haven’t been beneficial. If I find something isn’t working, I can eliminate it or try a different approach.

---

## The Two Core Principles of a Tracking System

The first is habit formation.

The second is adjusting your lifestyle to address specific issues.

With these two principles in mind, we can begin designing our own tracking system.

---

## I. Layout

I want to achieve the greatest results with the least amount of time and the simplest steps. I used to track things on a weekly basis, but having to redraw everything every week was a bit of a hassle. Plus, weekly records make it hard to see the overall trend.

So now I’ve switched to monthly tracking.

**Step 1: Identify the things that are most important to you and that you most want to track.**

Track what you care about. If you’re just starting out, you can also tell the AI about your current situation and your ideal state, and let it help you select a few tasks or habits that are best to start with. Make sure the tasks are small enough to start easily.

**My own tracking is roughly divided into three sections; I recommend writing them in the order they occur:**

The first section is learning. If, like me, the things you want to do or need to do vary from day to day, you can combine this with the Bullet Journal method. Start by drawing a small hollow circle to represent a plan. If you need to postpone it, draw an arrow on that circle to indicate the task has been moved. When the day actually comes to do it, draw a new small circle.

The second section is for life logging. For example, I used to pay close attention to my sleep patterns because I wanted to identify the factors causing my insomnia. If you want to change a habit—like cutting back on takeout—I recommend tracking the target behavior itself. For instance, record whether you cooked a meal rather than how many times you ordered takeout.

The third section is for tracking your physical condition, behavior, and emotions. Just prioritize recording the situations that occur most frequently. If nothing out of the ordinary happens, I’ll leave it blank.

Finally, mark weekends or days you’ve planned to rest, as well as special occasions. This helps prevent self-blame when you don’t meet your goals.

---

## II. Symbols

In many bullet journals, task completion is typically indicated by a checkmark or filling in a box. But this only shows whether a task was completed—I prefer to record how well it was done.

So I use circles of different sizes to indicate completion:

- Barely managed it → Draw a small dot
- Did a great job → Draw a slightly larger circle
- Got all the practice questions right → Turn the circle into a small flower

For example, I’ve been practicing the subjunctive mood recently. If there’s only one small flower, it means I haven’t fully mastered it yet.

You can also note your progress on the circle, such as writing the lesson number. By using a marker and a fountain pen together, you can convey more information within the same space.

I’ve also designed some simple little symbols to represent physical aspects, emotions, or behaviors.

If you want to track duration but aren’t using an auto-tracking app, you can actually use different symbols or colors to categorize.

But the simpler the symbols, the better—minimize barriers to capturing information as much as possible. If they’re too complex, they’ll actually increase the burden of recording and reviewing.

---

## III. Indexing

Simply tracking events only tells you what happened. But by adding an index, I can understand why.

Here are three methods I use myself:

**Method 1: Page Numbering.** This is the notebook’s built-in feature, which is great for reviewing, but it can be a bit cumbersome if you just want to quickly compare last month’s entries.

**Method 2: The Bullet Journal’s Cross-Reference Method. ** List the page numbers for the same topic together, so you can quickly jump between them for comparison.

**Method 3: Include the corresponding page numbers for each week in the monthly tracker.** When I’m reviewing my progress, if I notice any changes, I can immediately flip back to that week to see what happened at the time. When writing my journal, if I discover new changes, I’ll also flip back to check previous entries. If I confirm that a certain method is effective, I’ll continue using it—and even share it with others who might need it.
