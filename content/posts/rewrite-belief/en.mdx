When your energy starts to come back, what can you do next to get closer to the version of yourself you want to be?

Is our life really our own to decide?
Are our actions and beliefs today something we chose, or something we picked up unconsciously from our environment?

## Where do beliefs come from?

I often hear beautiful women say they're ugly.
I always give 100 and get 10 back—maybe I'm just not good at anything.
I don't deserve this. I'm not worthy.

Some of these beliefs come from other people, and some come from our own interpretation of events. At first they may show up only occasionally, but with repetition they stop feeling like a thought and start feeling like "this is just who I am."

Slowly they become our daily self-talk—even words we let slip out to others. Especially when we care a lot about how others see us, or when we've been in a long stretch of denial, these negative statements get taken as fact.

But wait—do our interpretations of events really have no other possibilities? Are these judgments from other people really you?

In fact, what others say about you is just them looking through you as a mirror, noticing what they happen to care about, and judging you with their own values. Up to that point, what they're reflecting is the speaker's inner world, not yours.

> **Them — (you) — what they care about, their standard**

A mirror doesn't need to be taken too seriously. If a lot of people give you similar feedback, write it down.

Of course, paying more attention to the negative isn't your fault—the brain is just designed that way. One bad thing really does carry more weight than one good thing.

## How a belief traps you

To save energy, the brain organizes past experience into a kind of "memory template."
Once I start believing "I'm unlucky,"
that template begins to shape the way I see the world.

Things that go wrong get replayed again and again,
while the moments that went smoothly are easy to miss.

At that point, the brain leans toward looking for evidence that confirms the belief.
This is called **confirmation bias**.

So—
the more I believe I'm unlucky,
the more easily I notice unlucky things.

In the end, the world I see really does start to look more and more like what I believe. That further reinforces the belief, and makes actions that contradict it harder and harder to take. It's very hard to act against a belief I still hold about myself.

## Methods for change

The brain likes to look for evidence, right? Then let's prepare it on purpose and show it some. A few small practices I'm using:

### 1. Gratitude journal

Recording helps us step out of the memory template and keep more of the evidence that would otherwise get filtered out. It doesn't have to be paper—any format works.

At first it felt really awkward—I couldn't find anything good at all. Later I found another angle: **if you'd feel something was missing without it, that's probably something you've gotten used to but is actually worth being grateful for.** Even one sentence is enough. The point is to repeat the small action.

By practicing gratitude, we reinforce positive thoughts. The more positive thoughts we have, the easier it becomes to notice things worth being grateful for—and a virtuous cycle forms.

### 2. Tag your achievements

Add a small "tag an achievement" move to whatever you already record. Not things outside your control like how many likes you got—only the things you genuinely did.

Plain recording only tells me what happened, but I'm a bit greedy—I want to squeeze out the full value. Actively tagging achievements re-tunes what I'm paying attention to.

### 3. Collect change

At first you may not notice your own change. Here are some directions to look. You can write it on the first page of a notebook, or if you already have a bullet journal, dedicate a special spot for it there. Stickers can break the page limit.

- Positive shifts in behavior or attitude
- Daily activities going more smoothly
- More effective communication
- Stronger problem-solving ability
- A new beginning
- A new skill learned
- Less negative emotion

You can also find someone who enjoys watching you change and grow, and share with them. But because the whole point is repetition, I still chose to write by hand.

These methods aren't really about changing you—they're about changing what you keep seeing on repeat.

### 4. Metacognition

Metacognition is a very powerful tool for managing negative thoughts. Simply put, it's being able to notice what we're thinking.

A simple way to train it: ask yourself, **what's my next thought**, then wait for it. Got it. Now, what's the next thought?

By recognizing and acknowledging negative thoughts, we can respond to them effectively. We can do it crying or laughing—it doesn't matter. There's no need to feel "I shouldn't be like this." I can resist while still taking some action—but that just drains more energy.

At the same time, I know I have a new choice. Do I want to take it now?
Do I want to challenge and reframe this belief?

Of course, we've gotten used to the old pattern, so trying to change all at once can feel a bit lost. You can find someone you look up to, or even ask an AI—what other ways are there to read this?

Just being able to recognize a thought or emotion is already enough to give us back a sense of agency. Notice the thought patterns you keep repeating—before, you might have slipped straight into an anxiety loop, but now you have a new angle. You know it's happening, and you can be aware of it. That alone is a very important change.
