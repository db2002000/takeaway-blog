I actually came across the Bullet Journal because a good friend of mine confused "Atomic Habits" with the Bullet Journal. But once I learned more about it, I realized it was a perfect fit for me.

In essence, it’s a personal management tool that combines to-do lists, planning, and reflection. It not only boosts productivity but also helps you gain a deeper understanding of yourself.

---

## The Core of Bullet Journaling: Symbols

One of the core elements of the Bullet Journal is the “bullet”—using symbols to distinguish different types of content.

Someone once asked me, “I have so many things I want to record—like a gratitude journal, reflections, and things that change every day—but I don’t know how to keep track of them all.”

Actually, you can categorize the content and create corresponding bullets.

The original creator used a dot to represent tasks, because a dot can easily be transformed into different shapes.

## My Note Structure

My notebook consists mainly of four parts: daily entries, weekly entries, monthly entries, and future entries.

### Weekly Entries

In the weekly section, I’ve divided the page into two sections. Most of the space is reserved for free-form daily entries, with different symbols used to distinguish specific categories.

If there are ideas or subtasks under a project, I indent them—this is the official Bullet Journal method.

How much space to allocate for daily entries depends on the situation, as daily needs can’t be predicted in advance. Once today’s entry is finished, I simply write the next day’s.

However, I think adding a bit of visual appeal is fun, so I pre-draw the left section—specifically for tracking reading and exact sleep times. While there are ways to incorporate this into a personal tracking system, I chose this method because I wanted to use stamps.

We could also turn this into a gratitude journal or use it for any other daily entries we want to keep.

Finally, I leave one box specifically for tasks I want to complete this week but haven’t decided on a specific day yet. There’s also a weekly review section where I note which parts I want to keep and which I want to improve—essentially a reflection section.

My layout isn’t actually that different; it’s just a matter of writing horizontally instead of vertically.

If there are special days—for example, if I’m skiing this week—I’ll draw an extra box specifically to record the weather, snow conditions, and so on for that day.

### Monthly Log

The original author’s layout is as follows: on the left is a calendar for recording key events—ideally after they’ve been completed, so it functions like a timeline. The other page is a task page where you list what you want to accomplish this month, which also helps you identify the most important items.

After reviewing everything, you can go back to last month’s entries to see which tasks weren’t finished and need to be carried over. If a particular item keeps getting carried over, perhaps it isn’t actually that important—could you delete it?

Personally, I present this in a calendar format. If I have to go out or am sick, I’ll use a marker to highlight the affected time period. For recurring tasks like video release dates, I’ll also note them on the calendar, leaving a blank space beside them to write down the task.

Another monthly record I keep is my [tracking system](/zh/posts/personal-tracking-system). It’s not an official Bullet Journal method, which is part of what makes the Bullet Journal so interesting—you can create any system you need.

## Future Log

The Bullet Journal is designed to address immediate needs, but sometimes we have tasks we want to complete that can’t be done right away. These can be placed in the Future Log, and when the time is right, we can move them—either from the Future Log to the present, or from the present to the Future Log.

Generally, the Future Log is placed after the Index and before your main journal entries, making it easier to find. You can also attach a sticker to it for quick reference.

---

## Standalone Pages

If an idea involves a large number of subtasks or contains additional related content, you can create a standalone page for it. This helps break down goals into individual components so you can tackle them one by one.

The content can vary widely, such as travel plans, challenges you’re facing, and more.

## Tracking System

The tracking system is actually part of a personalized collection, and its functionality lies in its timeliness—it’s not only useful in the present but should also provide value when you look back on it later.

Every time I reuse this template, I take a moment to consider what needs to be kept and what needs to be changed. It’s also important to maintain a good index so you can quickly find what you need when the time comes.
